# Book TOC Lab Knowledge

Core concepts for designing useful nonfiction before drafting.

## Overview

A useful nonfiction book behaves like a problem-solving product. Its promise, reader profile, scope, and table of contents should be designed before prose because those choices determine whether the book can deliver value and earn recommendations.

## Key Concepts

### Useful Book

**Definition**: A nonfiction book that helps a specific reader solve a real problem or reach a valued outcome.

Useful books can include memoir, history, philosophy, or reference if they are framed around a reader outcome. The point is not to make every book a how-to manual; the point is to make the value legible.

### Reader Promise

**Definition**: The concrete change the book credibly offers the reader.

The promise should be visible in the title, subtitle, cover, description, and TOC. A weak promise attracts the wrong readers and creates avoidable bad reviews.

### Scope

**Definition**: The book's boundary: promise, reader profile, who it is not for, and what it will not cover.

Strong scope is usually narrow. A book does not need to start from the beginning, serve everyone, or cover every adjacent topic.

### DEEP

**Definition**: A quality model for useful nonfiction: Desirable, Effective, Engaging, Polished.

The first two qualities dominate. If readers do not want the promise or the book does not work for them, polished prose and marketing cannot rescue it.

### Recommendation Loop

**Definition**: The situation where a reader's problem comes up naturally, another person recommends the book, and the book works well enough to create future recommendations.

Recommendability is easier to design when the reader problem is urgent, discussable, and tied to a recognizable person or context.

### Takeaway-First TOC

**Definition**: A table of contents whose chapter and section titles state reader outcomes, not author categories.

The TOC is a design artifact. It should reveal what the reader learns, in what order, and why each part belongs.

## Terminology

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| Promise | The useful outcome the book offers. |
| Reader profile | The specific type of reader the book is best for. |
| Out of scope | Tempting content deliberately excluded so the book can solve the main problem better. |
| Takeaway | What the reader can understand, decide, or do after a section. |
| Recommendation story | A short narrative explaining who recommends the book, to whom, and why. |

## How It Relates To

- **Reader-experience editing**: TOC design sets the first version of value pacing.
- **Beta-reader feedback**: Beta reading tests whether the promise, TOC, and manuscript work for real readers.
- **Seed marketing**: A strong recommendation loop makes early marketing compound instead of stay manual.

## Common Misconceptions

- **Myth**: A broader book has a larger market.
  **Reality**: Broad scope often makes the book less recommendable because nobody knows who it is best for.

- **Myth**: Clever chapter titles make the TOC more appealing.
  **Reality**: During design, descriptive takeaway titles are more useful because they expose missing value, weak sequence, and vague outcomes.

- **Myth**: Reader conversations should ask whether people like the book idea.
  **Reality**: Opinion questions invite compliments. Better conversations explore the reader's lived problem, decisions, failed attempts, language, and urgency.
