# Book TOC Lab Examples

Synthetic examples for applying the planning rules.

## Weak Scope To Strong Scope

### Weak

**Book**: A complete guide to remote work.

**Problems**:
- Reader profile is too broad.
- Promise is informational, not outcome-based.
- No clear exclusion line.
- Hard to recommend because many readers have different remote-work problems.

### Stronger

**Book**: A practical guide for first-time engineering managers who need to run effective one-on-ones with remote reports.

**Why it works**:
- Reader is specific.
- Situation is clear.
- Outcome is practical.
- Adjacent topics like hiring, compensation, and async documentation can be out of scope.

## Topic Title To Takeaway Title

### Weak TOC Titles

- Sales 101
- Research
- Remote Meetings
- Productivity Tools

### Stronger TOC Titles

- Ask Sales Questions That Reveal Budget, Urgency, And Risk
- Turn Raw Interviews Into A Short List Of Reader Objections
- Run Remote Meetings That End With One Owner Per Decision
- Choose Tools By Workflow Friction, Not Feature Count

**Why it works**:
- Each title implies a reader gain.
- The author can test whether the section actually delivers that gain.
- A reviewer can spot vague or duplicated sections earlier.

## Recommendation Story

### Weak

"People who like leadership books might recommend this to other leaders."

**Problems**:
- No trigger.
- No specific reader.
- No urgent problem.
- No reason this book beats alternatives.

### Stronger

"A new CTO complains that their first managers are running one-on-ones like status meetings. A more experienced CTO recommends this book because it gives exact scripts, meeting cadences, and failure signs for first-time engineering managers."

**Why it works**:
- The problem is discussable.
- The recommender knows exactly who needs it.
- The promise is specific enough to test.

## Reader Conversation Prompt

Use prompts about lived experience rather than book opinions:

```text
You dealt with [problem] recently, right? Could you walk me through what happened, what you tried, where you looked for help, and what still feels unresolved?
```

Avoid:

```text
I am writing a book about [topic]. Do you think that is a good idea?
```

**Why it works**:
- The first prompt reveals reader language, urgency, alternatives, and real obstacles.
- The second prompt invites politeness and speculative opinions.
