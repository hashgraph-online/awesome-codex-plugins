# Liking and Trust Examples

Examples are paraphrased applications of the principle, not book excerpts.

## Bad and Better Examples

### Cold email

Bad: 'I love your amazing company' with no evidence. Better: 'I noticed your team just launched X; the onboarding step at Y made me curious about Z.'

### Customer interview

Bad: Compliment the user's taste before asking leading questions. Better: thank them, state you want honest criticism, and ask behavior-based questions.

### Referral ask

Bad: 'Your friend said you need this.' Better: 'Sam suggested you might have context on X; no pressure if not relevant.'

## Rewrite Pattern

1. Identify the active cue.
2. Remove unsupported claims or coercive framing.
3. Add the real evidence, constraint, or user benefit.
4. Make the next step clear but optional.

## Mini Review Example

**Input**: A message uses pressure but does not show evidence.

**Output**:
- Principle: Liking and Trust
- Risk: unsupported cue may create a shortcut response.
- Rewrite: state the real reason, proof, or constraint; remove fake pressure.
