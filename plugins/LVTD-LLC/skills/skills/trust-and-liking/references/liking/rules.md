# Liking and Trust Rules

Rules for applying liking and trust without counterfeit evidence or coercive pressure.

## Core Rules

### 1. Use real common ground only

Mention similarity only when it is specific, relevant, and true.

### 2. Make praise precise and earned

Avoid generic flattery. Compliment observable work, choices, or outcomes.

### 3. Create cooperative framing

Position the interaction as solving a shared problem, not extracting compliance.

### 4. Protect discovery from bias

In interviews, rapport should make honesty easier, not steer answers toward approval.

## Guidelines

- Do not mirror, match, or flatter in ways that would feel embarrassing if disclosed.
- Prefer curiosity and usefulness over charm.
- In referral contexts, do not exploit the referrer's name beyond the permission they gave.

## Ethical Boundaries

- Use only honest evidence. Do not invent popularity, scarcity, credentials, endorsements, or social connection.
- Separate helping a good decision from pushing a shortcut response. If the cue is counterfeit, treat it as a red flag.
- When rewriting, preserve user agency: add context, alternatives, and enough time to decide when stakes are meaningful.

## Exceptions

- **Parody, critique, or analysis**: Describe manipulative tactics when the purpose is detection, education, or safer rewriting.
- **Real constraints**: Scarcity, authority, popularity, or reciprocity cues are acceptable when accurate, scoped, and relevant.
- **Tiny low-stakes choices**: Lighter disclosure may be fine, but never fabricate evidence.
