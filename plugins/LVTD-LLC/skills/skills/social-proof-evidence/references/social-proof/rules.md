# Social Proof Rules

Rules for applying social proof without counterfeit evidence or coercive pressure.

## Core Rules

### 1. Match proof to audience

Use proof from people or organizations similar to the target user.

### 2. Prefer specific proof over volume theater

A named case study or scoped metric usually beats vague popularity language.

### 3. Label proof accurately

Distinguish customer, beta user, applicant, subscriber, and visitor counts.

### 4. Do not stage popularity

Never create fake lines, fake counters, fake reviews, seeded donations, or implied consensus.

## Guidelines

- Use social proof to reduce uncertainty, not to silence evaluation.
- Avoid proof that suggests everyone agrees when the actual evidence is mixed.
- When proof is early or thin, say so and use qualitative specificity.

## Ethical Boundaries

- Use only honest evidence. Do not invent popularity, scarcity, credentials, endorsements, or social connection.
- Separate helping a good decision from pushing a shortcut response. If the cue is counterfeit, treat it as a red flag.
- When rewriting, preserve user agency: add context, alternatives, and enough time to decide when stakes are meaningful.

## Exceptions

- **Parody, critique, or analysis**: Describe manipulative tactics when the purpose is detection, education, or safer rewriting.
- **Real constraints**: Scarcity, authority, popularity, or reciprocity cues are acceptable when accurate, scoped, and relevant.
- **Tiny low-stakes choices**: Lighter disclosure may be fine, but never fabricate evidence.
