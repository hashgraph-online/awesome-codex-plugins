# Choose Social Proof Workflow

Select and present proof that is truthful, relevant, and not staged.

## When to Use

- Choosing testimonials, reviews, logos, case studies, or usage metrics
- Writing popularity, waitlist, community, or norm-based claims
- Auditing fake proof, staged proof, and pluralistic-ignorance risks

## Prerequisites

- The source material to create, rewrite, or audit.
- A clear target audience and desired action.
- Any evidence behind proof, urgency, authority, or relationship claims.

**Reference**: `references/social-proof/rules.md`

## Workflow Steps

### Step 1: Define the Decision

**Goal**: Identify what the audience is deciding and what would help them decide well.

- [ ] State the audience, context, and desired action.
- [ ] State the user's likely concern or uncertainty.
- [ ] Identify the real benefit or value exchange.

**Reference**: `references/social-proof/knowledge.md`

### Step 2: Identify Influence Cues

**Goal**: Name the cues before improving or critiquing them.

- [ ] Mark every cue related to social proof.
- [ ] Note any other Influence cues in play.
- [ ] Separate evidence from wording.

**Reference**: `references/social-proof/smells.md`

### Step 3: Verify Evidence and Boundaries

**Goal**: Keep useful persuasion and remove counterfeit cues.

- [ ] Verify each claim, constraint, credential, proof point, or relationship cue.
- [ ] Remove or rewrite unsupported cues.
- [ ] Surface material terms and tradeoffs near the ask.

**Reference**: `references/social-proof/rules.md`

### Step 4: Produce the Recommendation

**Goal**: Give an actionable output that preserves user agency.

- [ ] Explain the diagnosis in plain language.
- [ ] Provide the rewrite or design recommendation.
- [ ] Include caveats, evidence requirements, or follow-up questions only when needed.

**Reference**: `references/social-proof/examples.md`

### Step 5: Verify and Complete

**Goal**: Ensure the result is persuasive, honest, and usable.

- [ ] Run `references/social-proof/checklist.md`.
- [ ] Confirm no fake proof, fake scarcity, false authority, or obligation trap remains.
- [ ] Confirm the final ask is clear and optional.

## Quick Checklist

```
[ ] Decision defined
[ ] Influence cues named
[ ] Evidence verified
[ ] Unsupported pressure removed
[ ] Recommendation or rewrite delivered
[ ] Final checklist passed
```

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Is Bad | Do Instead |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Naming tactics without rewriting | Leaves the user with diagnosis but no usable next step | Provide a safer rewrite or design alternative |
| Removing all persuasion | Can make the message vague and less helpful | Keep truthful, relevant evidence |
| Treating conversion as the only metric | Can reward pressure that harms trust | Optimize for informed action and long-term credibility |

## Exit Criteria

- [ ] The relevant principle is named.
- [ ] Claims and cues are evidence-checked.
- [ ] Risks are stated without exaggeration.
- [ ] A concrete ethical rewrite or recommendation is provided.
