# Commitment and Consistency Rules

Rules for applying commitment and consistency without counterfeit evidence or coercive pressure.

## Core Rules

### 1. Begin with user-owned goals

Ask users to define what they want before asking for product commitment.

### 2. Use small commitments to clarify intent

A small action should reveal genuine preference, not create sunk-cost pressure.

### 3. Make terms stable before commitment

Do not secure a yes and then reveal material costs, obligations, or constraints.

### 4. Let users revise commitments

Authentic consistency includes changing course when new information matters.

## Guidelines

- Written commitments are useful for goals and plans; public commitments need care because they add reputational pressure.
- Effort should build competence or clarity, not just friction.
- Treat surprise fees, changed terms, and post-commitment pressure as lowball risks.

## Ethical Boundaries

- Use only honest evidence. Do not invent popularity, scarcity, credentials, endorsements, or social connection.
- Separate helping a good decision from pushing a shortcut response. If the cue is counterfeit, treat it as a red flag.
- When rewriting, preserve user agency: add context, alternatives, and enough time to decide when stakes are meaningful.

## Exceptions

- **Parody, critique, or analysis**: Describe manipulative tactics when the purpose is detection, education, or safer rewriting.
- **Real constraints**: Scarcity, authority, popularity, or reciprocity cues are acceptable when accurate, scoped, and relevant.
- **Tiny low-stakes choices**: Lighter disclosure may be fine, but never fabricate evidence.
