# Authority Rules

Rules for applying authority without counterfeit evidence or coercive pressure.

## Core Rules

### 1. Tie credentials to claims

Every authority signal should answer: expertise in what, for which claim, and with what limit?

### 2. Disclose role accurately

Advisor, investor, customer, reviewer, employee, and certifying body are different roles.

### 3. Avoid costume authority

Do not use visual symbols to imply review, endorsement, or expertise that is not present.

### 4. Require stronger evidence for high-stakes domains

Health, legal, financial, and safety claims need extra verification and cautious wording.

## Guidelines

- Use expert quotes for their actual domain, not general persuasion.
- Logos should mean a defined relationship; explain that relationship when ambiguity matters.
- When credentials are weak, prefer transparent experience over inflated authority language.

## Ethical Boundaries

- Use only honest evidence. Do not invent popularity, scarcity, credentials, endorsements, or social connection.
- Separate helping a good decision from pushing a shortcut response. If the cue is counterfeit, treat it as a red flag.
- When rewriting, preserve user agency: add context, alternatives, and enough time to decide when stakes are meaningful.

## Exceptions

- **Parody, critique, or analysis**: Describe manipulative tactics when the purpose is detection, education, or safer rewriting.
- **Real constraints**: Scarcity, authority, popularity, or reciprocity cues are acceptable when accurate, scoped, and relevant.
- **Tiny low-stakes choices**: Lighter disclosure may be fine, but never fabricate evidence.
