# Influence Audit Rules

Rules for applying influence audit without counterfeit evidence or coercive pressure.

## Core Rules

### 1. Name the cue before judging it

Classify each persuasion element as reciprocity, commitment, social proof, liking, authority, scarcity, contrast, or reason-giving before deciding whether it is ethical.

### 2. Verify the evidence behind the cue

Ask what real-world fact supports the claim. If the answer is missing, vague, or unverifiable, mark it as a counterfeit-cue risk.

### 3. Distinguish value from pressure

A strong offer can be persuasive without urgency or social evidence. Remove pressure cues that do not help the user evaluate value.

### 4. Rewrite by preserving the legitimate signal

Do not merely delete persuasion. Replace fake cues with truthful constraints, proof, qualifications, or neutral explanation.

## Guidelines

- Start with the highest-risk cues: scarcity, authority, social proof, and uninvited obligation.
- Treat vulnerable-user contexts, high-dollar purchases, health, legal, and financial claims as high scrutiny.
- When in doubt, recommend disclosure and slower decision paths.

## Ethical Boundaries

- Use only honest evidence. Do not invent popularity, scarcity, credentials, endorsements, or social connection.
- Separate helping a good decision from pushing a shortcut response. If the cue is counterfeit, treat it as a red flag.
- When rewriting, preserve user agency: add context, alternatives, and enough time to decide when stakes are meaningful.

## Exceptions

- **Parody, critique, or analysis**: Describe manipulative tactics when the purpose is detection, education, or safer rewriting.
- **Real constraints**: Scarcity, authority, popularity, or reciprocity cues are acceptable when accurate, scoped, and relevant.
- **Tiny low-stakes choices**: Lighter disclosure may be fine, but never fabricate evidence.
