# Experimentation Culture Rollout Knowledge

Core concepts for creating a test-friendly organization.

## Overview

Experimentation culture is the habit of using measured evidence to inform
product decisions. It depends on trust, incentives, data access, platform
usability, and a shared belief that learning is valuable even when a variant
does not win.

## Key Concepts

### Resistance

**Definition**: The organizational friction that prevents teams from testing
changes or trusting results.

Resistance may come from speed concerns, fear of negative results, distrust in
metrics, unclear ownership, or past experiences with burdensome process.

### Failed Experiment Myth

**Definition**: The belief that a neutral or negative experiment is a failure.

In a healthy experimentation culture, a non-winning test can still reduce risk,
explain user behavior, and improve future product decisions.

### First Rollout Slice

**Definition**: A small team, feature, or product surface chosen to prove the
value of experimentation before broader rollout.

The first slice should matter enough to create credibility but be small enough
to support closely.

### Demand Generation

**Definition**: Activities that make more teams want to use experimentation,
such as sharing readouts, templates, office hours, and internal success stories.

### Experimentation Cadence

**Definition**: A repeatable rhythm for planning, running, reading out, and
recording experiments.

Cadence turns experimentation from a one-off project into an operating habit.

## Adoption Metrics

| Metric | What It Shows |
|--------|---------------|
| Experiments launched | Whether teams are using the practice |
| Readouts completed | Whether tests produce decisions |
| Teams participating | Breadth of adoption |
| Time to launch | Process friction |
| Reused templates | Repeatability |
| Follow-up tests | Learning loop health |
